The Internet Infrastructure Tutorial

1. Autonomous Systems and Tiers

The internet is not a single network, but a network of networks. These networks are called Autonomous Systems (ASes), each managed by a single organization and identified by a unique ASN (Autonomous System Number). ASes are organized into tiers based on their connectivity and reach:
  • Tier-1 ASes: Global backbones with direct connections to other Tier-1 networks. They can reach every network on the internet without paying for transit. Examples include NTT, Tata Communications, and Bharti Airtel.
  • Tier-2 ASes: Regional ISPs that connect to Tier-1 networks and often peer with other Tier-2s. They serve large areas, aggregating traffic from Tier-3 networks. Examples include Verizon, Airtel Backbone, or RailTel.
  • Tier-3 ASes: Local ISPs that provide access to end-users, such as residents, businesses, or village networks. They connect to Tier-2 or sometimes directly to Tier-1 networks. Examples include Spectrum (NYC), Jio Access (Patna), or BSNL nodes in Arunachal Pradesh.
Understanding AS tiers is critical to visualizing how a data packet travels across the internet, from a local user to an international destination. Tier-3 handles local access, Tier-2 aggregates regionally, and Tier-1 carries traffic globally, often over submarine cables or international links.

2. Packet Journey: AT&T (NYC) → Jio (Patna)

Every packet sent over the internet follows a well-defined path through a hierarchy of networks called Autonomous Systems (ASes). Here’s how a packet travels from a user in Central Park, NYC to a Jio user in Patna, India:

1. The packet starts at the user’s device and enters the local ISP, in this case, Spectrum (Tier-3) in NYC. Tier-3 ISPs connect many end-users to the broader internet.

2. From there, the packet is routed to a regional Tier-2 ISP like Verizon, which aggregates traffic from multiple Tier-3 networks and connects to Tier-1 backbones.

3. The packet then enters a Tier-1 global backbone, such as NTT America, which has direct interconnections with other Tier-1 networks around the world. Tier-1 ASes form the spine of the internet.

4. To cross the Atlantic, the packet travels through a submarine cable (for example, the EIG cable) from the U.S. landing station (Bellport CLS) to India’s landing station (Mumbai CLS). Submarine cables carry nearly all intercontinental internet traffic.

5. Upon reaching India, the packet is handled by a Tier-1 Indian ISP like Bharti Airtel, which connects the international cable to the national backbone.

6. The packet moves through regional Tier-2 networks (Airtel Backbone) and finally reaches the local Tier-3 ISP in Patna (Jio Access) that serves the destination users.

7. Finally, the packet arrives at the end-users’ devices in Patna.

This journey highlights the hierarchical nature of the internet: Tier-3 connects users locally, Tier-2 aggregates regionally, and Tier-1 carries traffic globally. The undersea submarine cables are critical for international connectivity, forming the backbone of global communication.

3. Satellites & Remote Regions

Satellites are essential for connecting remote areas where fiber does not reach, such as deserts, mountains, or border villages. A villager’s Starlink dish (or VSAT terminal) beams data up to a LEO satellite.

That satellite must quickly downlink to the nearest licensed ground gateway in the same country (for example, Riyadh or Dubai for Saudi Arabia). From there, traffic flows into the national Tier-1/Tier-2 ISPs.

But here’s the key: satellites don’t replace the global internet backbone. They only bridge the "first mile" from remote villages to the backbone. Once traffic hits the gateway, it usually rides submarine cables — such as Jeddah → Mumbai or Chennai — to reach India’s internet core.

In India, packets land at Mumbai or Chennai Cable Landing Stations, then traverse Tier-1 carriers (Tata, Airtel, Jio), move onto regional Tier-2 backbones (RailTel, BSNL, Sify), and finally reach the local Tier-3 ISP or NESAC node in Arunachal Pradesh that serves the destination village.

So even with satellites, the submarine cables remain the backbone of the global internet. Satellites just provide access where fiber cannot.
